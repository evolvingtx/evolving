About

What Is EvoSensory?

EvoSensory is a multi-sensory rhythmic timing training platform I built as a pediatric OT because I couldn't find a tool that delivered real-time, clinically meaningful feedback without a significant equipment investment or subscription fee. It runs entirely in a web browser. A device and a pair of wired earbuds give you the full therapeutic loop.

The core of the app is the Micro-Timing Drift Engine (MTDE) — a real-time algorithm that tracks the slope of your timing errors across consecutive beats using linear regression. It doesn't just tell you if a tap was early or late. It tells you whether your timing is stabilizing, accelerating, or dragging — and adapts the audio and haptic feedback accordingly. That multi-beat trend analysis is not available in basic metronome-based software.

Rhythmic timing training has a strong evidence base across ADHD, DCD, dyslexia, TBI, cerebral palsy, and general sensory processing challenges. The research consistently shows that structured, precise metronome-based feedback improves bilateral coordination, attention, motor planning, processing speed, and reading. EvoSensory puts that clinical approach in a free, no-install tool that works from a school Chromebook or a $40 Android tablet.

The Platform

Four Training Modules

🎮
Classic Active
Core timing engine. Six progressive levels from bilateral alternation to complex rest patterns. Three visual modes: Falling Notes, Bubble Blast, Target Range. Spatial audio error correction (left ear = early, right ear = late), XAC haptic pillow integration, Blind Training mode, and adaptive hit window.
👁️
Oculomotor Active
Four sub-modes: smooth pursuits, saccadic jumps, depth pulse (convergence and accommodation), and gaze hold — all synced to the beat. Tap-based proxy tracking. Optional camera-based gaze estimation on Chrome (Phase 2). Screening-level data, clearly labeled.
🧠
Cognitive Active
Vocabulary and math embedded in the timing task. Speech is the beat: words spelled by syllable and spoken aloud, timed to the metronome. Math mode counts, adds, and multiplies. Targets phonological processing, number fluency, and auditory-motor integration simultaneously.
📷
Motion — Rhythm Training Beta
MediaPipe Pose (33 body landmarks) detects specific gestures timed to the beat. Four sub-modes: bilateral clapping, alternating knee taps with midline crossing, marching, and jumps. Live skeleton overlay, camera positioning guide, and auto-start when body is detected in frame. Chrome on Windows, Mac, or Android.
🎯
Active Games Beta
Five camera-based games controlled by body movement. Catch falling stars by side-stepping (vestibular, dynamic balance). Pop balloons by reaching overhead (shoulder ROM, visual-motor integration). Kick soccer balls with the matching foot (unilateral lower extremity, single-leg balance). Duck or jump to dodge obstacles (reaction time, whole-body motor planning). Hold balance zones for score multipliers (static balance, proprioception). Chrome on a computer required.
👁️ Eye Tracking — Phase 2 (Oculomotor Module)
An optional camera-based gaze estimation toggle is available in the Oculomotor sub-mode panel. When activated, it uses the front camera to estimate where the child's eyes are pointing during pursuits and saccades, overlaying a real-time gaze crosshair on the training canvas. Average gaze lag is recorded in the session data and included in the SOAP note.

Important disclosure: This is screening-level data (±60–80px), not clinical-grade eye movement recording. It is clearly labeled in the UI and SOAP note. It does not replace a functional vision or behavioral optometry evaluation.

The Feedback Loop

Three Simultaneous Channels

🔊
Auditory — Spatial: Early taps send an ascending tone to the left ear. Late taps send a descending tone to the right ear. The further from the beat, the further the stereo pan. The MTDE shapes the tone based on whether your timing is accelerating (faster chirp) or dragging (deeper slide). This is why wired headphones matter — Bluetooth audio lag defeats the feedback loop entirely.
📳
Haptic — Tactile: Device vibration fires with distinct patterns for early, late, and perfect taps. With the Xbox Adaptive Controller (XAC), left and right rumble channels fire independently — left side for early, right side for late. Designed to drive a haptic cushion for whole-body proprioceptive input. Audio haptic fallback active for iPhone users.
👁️
Visual — Gamified: Five visual game modes across Classic and Oculomotor modules, plus two Cognitive modes. All visuals are prototype-level — a full design polish pass is planned. Blind Training mode fades the canvas after 10 seconds to train pure auditory/haptic timing without visual anchoring.

Who It's For

Pediatric and Adult Populations

EvoSensory was built in pediatric OT practice but the clinical applications extend across the lifespan. Any population with timing, coordination, attention, or motor planning deficits can benefit. Current user profiles support three age categories — Pediatric (0–17), Adult (18–64), and Older Adult (65+) — with BPM defaults, hit window settings, and SOAP note language that adjust automatically.

Populations with documented evidence for rhythmic timing intervention: ADHD, DCD, dyslexia, autism, TBI, stroke, Parkinson's disease, MS, cerebral palsy, sensory processing disorder, anxiety, and age-related motor and cognitive decline. The Motion and Active Games modules extend reach to standing balance, gait, vestibular processing, and upper and lower extremity coordination.

Reading the Data

Understanding the Results

EvoSensory tracks three separate things after every session. They measure different skills and should not be confused with each other.

1. Accuracy — Were they hitting the beat at all?

Accuracy is the percentage of beats the patient responded to within the acceptable time window. A patient with 90% accuracy hit the window on 9 out of 10 beats. This reflects engagement, attention, and whether the task level is appropriate.

2. Timing Deviation — Where exactly were their taps landing?

Deviation is measured in milliseconds (ms) and shows the average distance between where the beat was and where the tap landed. Lower is better — zero means perfectly on the beat. The sign tells you direction: positive (+) means early (tapping before the beat), negative (−) means late (tapping after the beat).

A patient can score 95% accuracy but still have a +55ms average deviation — meaning they hit the window every time, but they are consistently half a beat early. A therapist watching the session might say "great job." The app captures that the patient is systematically early every single tap. That is a different clinical finding than accuracy alone can show.

3. Session Trend — Did their timing change as the session went on?

The session trend tracks whether errors got bigger, smaller, or stayed the same as the session progressed. A patient who starts well and drifts earlier over time may have difficulty sustaining inhibitory control. One who drifts later may be experiencing attention fatigue or motor planning difficulty under sustained demand.

For Therapists

Built for Real Clinical Documentation

EvoSensory works across home, school, clinic, and telehealth settings. Every session generates objective, reportable data: accuracy percentage, average timing deviation in milliseconds, session trend (stable / drifting early / drifting late), oculomotor mode breakdown, and optional camera-based gaze screening when eye tracking is active. That data is designed to support skilled service justification in your documentation.

The SOAP note generator builds a complete draft note after each session with plain-language interpretation of all three metrics, modality and age category context, hardware confirmation, next-session recommendations, and appropriate disclosures. Edit before pasting into your EMR. Always labeled as a draft.

Multiple client profiles can be tracked on a single device. Progress charts display accuracy and average deviation across the last 15 sessions per user. CSV export available for external analysis or parent sharing.
