The Evidence

Why Rhythmic Timing Training Works

Rhythmic timing training has a substantial evidence base built across more than three decades of research in neuroscience, occupational therapy, physical therapy, and special education. The mechanism is well understood: the brain's internal timing system — involving the cerebellum, basal ganglia, supplementary motor area, and prefrontal cortex — can be trained through structured, repetitive practice at synchronizing movement to an external beat. EvoSensory is a delivery tool for that evidence base.

Which module is evidence-based vs. evidence-informed?

EvoSensory Classic is the only module with direct grounding in the rhythmic timing training evidence base. It implements the specific mechanism — voluntary movement synchronized to an external auditory beat with immediate multimodal error feedback — that the peer-reviewed literature has measured and validated across ADHD, DCD, dyslexia, TBI, stroke, Parkinson's, and handwriting.

The other four modules are evidence-informed extensions. Each applies timing training principles to an additional clinical domain (visual-motor, phonological, whole-body motor, game-based motor) that has its own research support, but the specific combined approach has not been independently validated as a clinical protocol. Use Classic as the primary intervention and the other modules as complements when the target domain is clinically relevant.

The Internal Timing System

The cerebellum and basal ganglia work together to predict when a beat will arrive, prepare a motor response in advance, and execute it at the right moment. When this system is less efficient — as seen in ADHD, DCD, TBI, stroke, and many other neurological and developmental conditions — timing errors are not random. They are systematic: children with ADHD tend to tap early (anticipatory motor response), while children with DCD or processing speed difficulties tend to tap late (delayed initiation). EvoSensory measures both and distinguishes between them.

Why Millisecond Precision Matters

A therapist watching a session can observe whether a child is hitting the beat or not. What they cannot observe with the naked eye is whether every tap is 8ms early versus 62ms early — a difference that is clinically meaningful and diagnostically significant. The 8ms patient has an established internal clock with minor fine-tuning needed. The 62ms patient has a systematic anticipatory pattern that directly mirrors impulsivity in daily functioning. EvoSensory makes that distinction visible and documentable.

The gap this fills

Most evidence-based timing interventions require expensive clinic equipment, trained administrators, and per-session licensing fees that make home programs impractical. EvoSensory delivers the same core mechanism — structured rhythmic feedback with immediate error correction — through a free browser-based tool that works on any device. The goal is to make the between-session repetition that drives neural adaptation actually accessible to the families who need it.

Step by Step

Quick Start

1
Open the app and create a patient profile
Go to www.evolve-ot.com/evosensory.html in Chrome. Tap + New Player. Enter name or initials, select age category, choose training modality. The app sets a starting BPM automatically. Bookmark the page or tap Share → Add to Home Screen for easy access next session.
2
Plug in wired headphones
Left ear tone = tapping early. Right ear tone = tapping late. This is the core clinical mechanism — Bluetooth adds 100–200ms of lag that defeats it entirely. Any wired earbuds work, including old phone earbuds. Do this before starting any session.
3
Choose a module and level
Select Classic → Level 0 (First Beat) for patients new to the tool, or Level 1 (Warm Up) for patients with some prior rhythm experience. Classic is the evidence-based core — start every patient here. Adjust BPM with the + / − buttons. See the Module Guide for when to add other modules.
4
Confirm hardware
A setup screen appears before each session. Check off what's connected — wired headphones, device vibration, XAC cushion. Only what you confirm is documented in the SOAP note.
5
Tap with the beat
Tap the large button, tap the canvas, or press spacebar on desktop. One tap per beat. The app scores every response in real time.
6
Review results and generate a SOAP note
When the session ends, results appear automatically. Tap Generate SOAP Note to get a pre-populated draft. Copy to clipboard or print. Edit before pasting into your EMR.

Why feet start slower

Foot tap BPM is set 8–10 BPM lower than hand BPM because the motor sequence from brain signal to floor contact is longer — hip, knee, ankle, and foot must all sequence before contact. The app adjusts automatically when a foot modality is selected.

BPM progression rule

Increase tempo by 4–8 BPM at a time. Accuracy at a slow, steady tempo is the foundation for everything else. If accuracy drops below 50%, reduce BPM before changing the level. The app tightens the hit window automatically as the patient improves across sessions.

Data

Reading the Results

EvoSensory tracks three separate things after every session. They measure different skills and should not be confused with each other.

1. Accuracy — Were they hitting the beat?

The percentage of beats the patient responded to within the acceptable time window. Reflects engagement, attention, and whether the task level is appropriate. High accuracy alone does not mean good timing.

2. Timing Deviation — Where exactly did their taps land?

Measured in milliseconds (ms). Shows the average distance between the beat and where the tap landed. Lower is better. Zero = perfectly on the beat.

The sign tells you direction: positive (+) = tapping early (before the beat). Positive (+) = tapping late (after the beat).

Accuracy and deviation are not the same

A patient can score 95% accuracy but have a +55ms average deviation — hitting the window every time, but always early. A therapist watching might say "great job." The app captures that the patient is systematically early on every single tap. That is a separate and clinically meaningful finding that accuracy alone cannot show.

3. Session Trend — Did timing change during the session?

Tracks whether errors got bigger, smaller, or stayed the same as the session progressed. A patient who starts well and drifts earlier over time may have difficulty sustaining inhibitory control. One who drifts later may be experiencing attention fatigue.

Key documentation point

Always report both accuracy and deviation. A high accuracy score with significant deviation is still a clinically meaningful finding. Connect the deviation direction to a functional impact in the patient's daily occupations.

Documentation

SOAP Note Guide

After every session, tap Generate SOAP Note on the results screen or from the stats screen (last session). The note is pre-populated with all session data. It includes a "how to read this note" footnote at the bottom for reviewers unfamiliar with the tool.

What's in Each Section

SectionContent

OAccuracy %, timing deviation with direction, session trend, level, BPM, modality, age category, sensory hardware confirmed

Always edit before use

The SOAP note is a draft. The A section includes fill-in prompts (e.g., "connect to functional occupation — handwriting, ADLs, social participation"). Review and personalize every note before pasting into your EMR or submitting for billing.

Tips for Insurance Documentation

The note is designed to support skilled service justification. The most important things to add in the A section are the functional connections — how does this patient's timing deficit actually show up in handwriting, dressing, reading, turn-taking, or gait? The objective numbers are already there. The insurance reviewer needs to see why those numbers matter for daily life.

Setup

Calibration

Eye Tracking Calibration (WebGazer)

Eye tracking is an optional toggle in the Oculomotor module panel. When turned on, it uses your front camera to estimate where the patient's eyes are pointing during pursuits and saccades. It is a screening-level tool — not clinical-grade eye movement recording.

Requirements: Chrome on a Windows, Mac, or Android computer. Not supported on iPhone, iPad, or Safari.

How to calibrate: Enable the toggle, then tap Play. A 9-point calibration grid appears. Look at each numbered dot and tap or click it. Once all 9 are calibrated, tap Start Session. The gaze crosshair appears on the canvas during the session.

Accuracy note

WebGazer accuracy is approximately ±60–80 pixels depending on device, lighting, and head position. This is screening-level — enough to see whether a patient is clearly trailing behind or anticipating a target, but not precise enough to replace a behavioral optometry evaluation. The SOAP note includes appropriate disclosure language automatically.

XAC setup

Your existing AAC switch plugs directly into the 3.5mm back jacks on the XAC — no adapter needed. Button 0 = LEFT tap, Button 1 = RIGHT tap. Place a haptic cushion on the left and right sides of the chair seat. Left side fires when the patient taps early; right side fires when late. The patient feels timing correction through their body.

Foot tap setup

Any USB switch or jelly bean works as a foot tap button in Classic mode. Level 6 is specifically designed for bilateral foot tap training. The app automatically applies lower starting BPM for foot modality profiles.
